ENIGMA — FIELD MANUAL & HISTORY

Geheime Kommandosache · For Authorised Personnel Only

Origins & Development

The Enigma machine was a series of electro-mechanical rotor cipher devices developed and used primarily in the early-to-mid 20th century for commercial and military communication secrecy. The earliest version was invented by German engineer Arthur Scherbius in 1918, who filed his first patent in February 1918 — just months before the end of World War I. It was originally marketed as a commercial encryption tool for businesses.

The German military — first the Reichswehr and later the Wehrmacht — began adopting Enigma in the late 1920s. By World War II, the machine had evolved through several generations and was in wide use across the Army (Heer), Navy (Kriegsmarine), Air Force (Luftwaffe), and intelligence services.

Physical Description

The standard military Enigma (Enigma I, used by the Army and Air Force) was a portable wooden or metal box roughly the size of a typewriter, weighing approximately 12 kg (26 lbs) with its carrying case. It contained:

  • A keyboard of 26 keys arranged in the German QWERTZ layout
  • A lampboard above the keyboard, with 26 bulbs corresponding to each letter
  • A rotor assembly of 3 (later 4) rotors mounted in a horizontal slot
  • A reflector (Umkehrwalze / UKW) at the left end of the rotor assembly
  • A plugboard (Steckerbrett) on the front face, allowing cable patch connections
  • A battery providing the electrical current

Operators would press a key, and the current would light up a lamp showing the enciphered letter. A second operator recorded each lit letter. Messages were then transmitted by radio in Morse code.

The Battle of Wits — Breaking Enigma

Polish mathematicians — Marian Rejewski, Jerzy Różycki, and Henryk Zygalski — were the first to crack Enigma mathematically, in 1932. They exploited procedural weaknesses in how German operators used the machine and shared their findings with British and French intelligence just before the war.

At Bletchley Park, Britain assembled a team of mathematicians, linguists, and crossword enthusiasts — including Alan Turing and Gordon Welchman — who developed the electromechanical Bombe, a device that could rapidly test rotor configurations to find valid settings for a given day's traffic.

The intelligence produced from broken Enigma traffic was codenamed ULTRA and is considered one of the greatest intelligence achievements in history. Churchill reportedly told King George VI that it was ULTRA that won the war. Historians estimate it shortened the war by 2–4 years and saved millions of lives.

Legacy

After the war, Enigma's full story remained classified until the 1970s. The machines were secretly sold to developing nations who didn't know the Allies could read their communications for decades. Today, original Enigma machines are rare museum pieces and collector items, some selling at auction for over $400,000 USD.

Enigma's legacy lives on in modern cryptography — its failure taught the world that procedural discipline matters as much as mathematical strength, and that no cipher is unbreakable if its operators make mistakes.

The Substitution Principle

Enigma is a polyalphabetic substitution cipher — each keypress substitutes one letter for another, but the substitution alphabet changes with every single keystroke. This is what makes it far stronger than a simple Caesar cipher.

When you press a key, an electrical signal travels through the machine along this path:

KEY → PLUGBOARD IN → RIGHT ROTOR → MIDDLE ROTOR → LEFT ROTOR → REFLECTOR → LEFT ROTOR → MIDDLE ROTOR → RIGHT ROTOR → PLUGBOARD OUT → LAMP

The Rotors

Each rotor is a disc with 26 electrical contacts on each face, wired in a scrambled pattern. The wiring was fixed at manufacture — each historical rotor (I through V) had a unique wiring. When a rotor is in position A, contact 1 might connect to contact 5 on the other side; in position B, the disc has rotated, shifting all connections by one.

The right rotor advances one step with every keypress — like the ones column in an odometer. When the right rotor completes a full revolution (or reaches its notch position), it causes the middle rotor to advance. The middle rotor similarly triggers the left rotor.

The famous double-stepping anomaly: due to the mechanical design, the middle rotor steps twice in certain configurations — once when it causes the left rotor to advance, and again on the next keypress. This simulation replicates this authentic quirk.

The Reflector (Umkehrwalze)

The reflector is a non-rotating disc at the left end of the rotor assembly. It pairs each letter with another and sends the signal back through the rotors in reverse. The reflector serves two purposes: it creates a reciprocal cipher (if A encrypts to Z, then Z encrypts to A with the same settings), and it means a letter can never encrypt to itself — a critical weakness that Bletchley exploited.

The Plugboard (Steckerbrett)

Added to military Enigmas in 1930, the plugboard allowed operators to swap pairs of letters using patch cables before and after the rotor scrambling. With 10 cables connecting 20 letters into 10 pairs, the plugboard multiplied the number of possible machine configurations by a factor of approximately 150 trillion. It was the single largest contributor to Enigma's overall key space.

Ring Settings (Ringstellung)

Each rotor has an outer ring with the alphabet printed on it. The ring setting controls the offset between the outer ring's alphabet and the rotor's internal wiring. This adds another layer of complexity and was part of each day's key settings distributed to operators.

Quick Start

  1. Both sender and receiver must configure their machines identically before communicating.
  2. Set the rotor types using the dropdown under each drum (e.g. I, II, III).
  3. Set the ring settings (Ringstellung) using the number input below each rotor selector.
  4. Set the starting positions using the ▲▼ buttons or by clicking the drum to step forward.
  5. Wire up to 10 plugboard pairs by clicking any two sockets on the Steckerbrett.
  6. Select your reflector (UKW-B is standard Wehrmacht; UKW-C was used in some contexts).
  7. Type your message using the on-screen keyboard or your physical keyboard.
  8. Read the encrypted output from the CHIFFRE AUSGABE panel.

Encrypting a Message

Type directly using your keyboard or click the on-screen keys. Each keypress advances the rotors and lights a lamp showing the ciphertext letter. The output panel groups letters in blocks of 5 — this was standard practice to make Morse transmission easier and to obscure word boundaries.

You can also paste or type in the Plaintext Input textarea and press ENCRYPT/DECRYPT to process the full message at once. Note: non-alphabetic characters are ignored (spaces, numbers, punctuation).

Decrypting a Message

Enigma is self-reciprocal: to decrypt, simply configure the machine to the exact same settings used to encrypt, then type (or paste) the ciphertext. The output will be the original plaintext. This is Enigma's most elegant property — the same machine and same settings work in both directions.

Use RESET POSITIONS to return rotors to AAA (000) and clear the output before starting a new decryption session.

Historical Operating Procedure

Real German operators followed strict daily key sheets (Schlüsselheft) distributed in advance. Each day had a new set of rotor order, ring settings, plugboard connections, and a ground setting. The operator would first set the machine to the day's ground settings, then type an indicator (a chosen 3-letter starting position, repeated twice as an error check), which the receiver used to set their rotors before decoding the actual message. Many Enigma breaks at Bletchley came from operators choosing predictable indicators (like their girlfriend's initials or "AAA") or from lazy re-use of settings.

What This Simulation Simplifies or Omits

  • ✘ Only 3 rotors — The Kriegsmarine (Navy) used a 4-rotor Enigma (M4) with an extra thin reflector and a special 4th rotor (Beta or Gamma). This simulation uses 3 rotors only.
  • ✘ No rotor lock-in during keypress — On the real machine, the rotors advanced before the electrical circuit closed (pre-advance mechanism). This simulation steps first and then encrypts, which is functionally equivalent but mechanically simplified.
  • ✘ No printer tape — Some Enigma models (particularly the Enigma T and railway variants) had a built-in letter printer that produced a paper tape. This simulation displays output on screen instead.
  • ✘ No 4th rotor slot — The simulation does not support Navy M4 configuration with Beta/Gamma rotors.
  • ✘ Plug cable visuals — Real plugboard cables were color-coded physical wires. This simulation shows sockets with green LED indicators and a text list of pairs, without visual cable routing.
  • ✘ No key indicator protocol — The historical two-part message process (ground settings → indicator → message key) is not enforced. You configure settings freely.
  • ✘ No power switch or lid — The original machine had a hinged lid over the lampboard to shade the bulbs in daylight and a physical on/off switch. Not simulated here.
  • ✘ Unlimited plug pairs — The simulation allows up to 13 pairs (any two sockets). Historically, exactly 10 cables were used as operational practice. The simulation does not enforce this limit.
  • ✘ No Morse code transmission layer — Real Enigma output was transmitted by radio operators as Morse code. The text output here skips that layer entirely.

Cryptographic Accuracy

The encryption algorithm implemented here is mathematically identical to the Wehrmacht Enigma I. Messages encrypted with this simulation using the same settings will produce the same ciphertext as a real Enigma I machine or any other accurate software simulation.
